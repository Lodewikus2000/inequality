Income inequality around the world

Ever since Piketty published Capital in the Twenty-First Century (2013), income inequality is at the center of public debate. A lot of data is available on this subject. This page makes an attempt to give some insight into this data in a way that is easy to understand.

Compare the countries

This world map shows how much of the national income is obtained by the top 10%, top 1%, or bottom 10% in several countries. As you can see, the top 10% in Europe does not gather as much of the GDP as they do in, for example, Arab countries or the USA. You will also notice that the bottom 10% in Europe takes a relatively large piece of the pie, compared to other parts of the world.

Slicing the pie

The pie represents the GDP of the selected country in the selected year; the incomes of all people combined. The pie consists of 10 pieces, each piece belonging to 10% of the population. See how the sizes of these pieces are different across countries and time. In some countries, the top 10% gathers more than half of all income, like in Qatar.

Move the mouse over a pie piece (or a person) to see the exact percentage and the average income in that group.

Press the button to see how the pieces are distributed across the 10 income groups.

The Lorenz curve

A Lorenz curve is an informative way of showing the income distribution.

The Lorenz curve shows how much of the income is earned by the bottom X %. In a completely equal society, 20% of the people would earn 20% of the income, and 78% of the people would earn 78% of the income, and so on. The curve would then be a perfectly diagonal line. The more the real curve deviates from this diagonal, the more unequal a society's income is distributed.

Change over time

This line graph displays the size of the shares of all income groups across time.

You will see that in most countries, the top 10% increased their share of the income in the last decades. An especially interesting (but not surprising) spike in the share of the top 10% can be found in Russia after the collapse of the Soviet Union. In Saudi Arabia, on the other hand, nothing ever changes at all.
